So you want me to build your website… Now what?

So you want me to build your website? Well, you’ve come to the right place. But here’s the thing… Starting a business is a huge commitment. Choosing to build a website is an equally daunting responsibility. And unless you have fully prepared yourself for the journey ahead, I can’t help you.

When a potential client reaches out to me, I always reply with a lengthy response. I ask, what I assume are to them, the most annoyingly cumbersome questions. Things like, “have you chosen a color scheme?” Or, “have you identified your target audience?” They are simple questions, but they carry a lot of weight.

It’s My Job!

For many of my clients, I find that few of them have thought about color psychology and its correlation to web design. And that’s perfectly fine. Some of them aren’t aware that Flash is an outdated practice either. And unless my client also happens to be a web designer and/or developer, then it’s not their job to know that. But it is mines.

When I write those wordy questionnaire -eqsue emails, I don’t do it for my own amusement. I do it because it’s my job.

I’m supposed to know that one page websites tend to yield higher conversions. I’m also supposed to know that adding audio with autoplay, that blares in to the ears of your visitors the moment that they land on your page, is a huge faux pas (note that this extends to video as well).

I don’t expect my clients to know any of this stuff, and why should they? Their job is to manage their business, or sell a product, or showcase their talents. My job is to ensure that they have the best possible website to do so with. But herein lies the issue. 

The Problem Is….


Not often, but on occasion, my long-winded responses are met with simple one word replies. These replies do not answer basic questions such as, “have you acquired a domain name and hosting yet?” Instead, they serve as a response to a question that I didn’t ask: are you serious about this?

Plenty of people set out to build their own website without doing any planning. In fact, I’ve done it so much that I’ve become an expert of sorts at recognizing its beginning stages. However it was my repeated failure at building a successful website of my own, that taught me the do’s and dont’s of the conceptual phase.

As a predominantly self-taught designer and developer, I’ve had to fall and fumble a lot, just to learn a little. But it was worth it, because what I have learned, I am able to implement with my clients. Which is why I ask my potential clients so many questions; to ensure that they don’t make the same mistakes that I first did.

Now What?

So if you want me to build your website, I can do it. In fact, I’d love to do it. But I won’t… not if you aren’t willing to plan and conceptualize first. I know how that sounds. Shouldn’t I want your money? Shouldn’t I chase after willing, paying clients? Of course I should, and I do. Just not for clients who show me that they aren’t committed.

I need my clients to do a little legwork too. And that means answering emails. Not with dismissive one liners that fail to address any of my concerns, but with a well thought out response that, at the very least, gives me a solid understanding of your goals. I need to know what you plan to accomplish with your website. But before you tell me, tell yourself. Because I can’t help you to reach your goals if you don’t know what they are.
